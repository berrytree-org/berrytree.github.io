## Chapter 15

# John Robert Berry 1842–1917

John Robert Berry was born on September 25th, 1842, and was the third son of Thomas Person and Sarah Lunsford Berry. He is also my great-grandfather. He fought in the Civil War and was wounded in the battle of Cold Harbor. After the war, he returned to Orange County, North Carolina, and married Elizabeth Frances Bowling on November 30th, 1865.

## John Robert Berry Enlists in the Confederate Army

The Civil War began on April 12th, 1861, and John Robert Berry enlisted as a private in the Confederate Army on October 6, 1861. He was placed in Company E, 31st Infantry Regiment of North Carolina. He was only 19 years old at the time.

**Service Timeline:**

- Enlisted in Company E, 31st Infantry Regiment, North Carolina, October 6, 1861
- POW on February 8, 1862, at Roanoke Island, NC
- Paroled on February 21, 1862, at Elizabeth City, NC
- Returned September 15, 1862
- POW on June 1, 1864, at Cold Harbor, VA
- Wounded on June 1, 1864, at Cold Harbor, VA
- Confined on August 16, 1864, at Old Capitol Prison, Washington, DC
- Transferred on August 27, 1864, to Elmira, NY
- Took Oath of Allegiance on June 23, 1865, at Elmira, NY. Returned to North Carolina

## Elmira, New York, Union Prison

John Robert Berry was wounded on the second day of the battle at Cold Harbor in Richmond, Virginia. He was wounded on his right hand and shoulder. He was captured on May 31st or June 1st, 1864. John Robert Berry was then sent to a hospital in Washington, D.C., for about a month and a half. On August 16th, 1864, he was transferred to Elmira, New York. This was John Robert's residence from August 27th, 1864, until the conclusion of the war. He was released on the 23rd of June, 1865. After taking an Oath of Allegiance to the Constitution of the United States of America, John Robert Berry was put on a train to Hillsborough, NC. After arriving late in the day, John Robert Berry had to walk about 14 miles to his home at Fiddleton. He arrived before about 4:00 AM and rather than wake his parents, he lay down on a log bench across the road from their house to sleep until daybreak. This was probably a good idea, because people were still a bit jumpy and traumatized by the war and being shot at night was a real possibility.
